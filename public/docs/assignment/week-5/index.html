    Week 05 – 3D Printing and Scanning#

    The assignment for this week was to:

    • Design, document, and 3D print an object that could not be made subtractively
    • 3D scan an object and optionally 3D print it

    Group Assignment#

    🔗 https://fabacademy.org/2026/labs/kochi/group_assignmetns/week05/

    Testing the Design Rules for Our 3D Printers#

    For the group assignment, we focused on understanding the limitations and capabilities of our 3D printers by printing standardized test models.

    Some of the key parameters we evaluated:

    • Overhang angles
    • Bridging distances
    • Wall thickness
    • Clearances / tolerances
    • Support performance
    • Dimensional accuracy

    This testing helped us determine:

    • The maximum unsupported bridge length
    • The maximum printable overhang angle without support
    • Minimum feature sizes
    • Tolerance values for press-fit or movable assemblies

    Key Findings#

    • Bridges performed reliably up to ~20 mm without sagging.
    • Overhangs were clean up to around 45° without support.
    • Clearances below certain thresholds resulted in fused parts.

    These results directly influenced the design decisions in my individual assignment.

    Quick Comparison: Subtractive vs Additive Manufacturing#

    Subtractive Manufacturing Additive Manufacturing
    Material is removed from a solid block Material is added layer by layer
    Examples: CNC milling, turning Example: FDM 3D printing
    Limited by tool access Can create complex internal geometries
    More material waste Minimal waste
    Stronger bulk properties (depending on process) Layer-dependent strength

    Why This Matters#

    Subtractive methods cannot create fully enclosed internal cavities, complex lattices, or interlocked parts in one piece without assembly.

    Additive manufacturing enables:

    • Internal lattice structures
    • Print-in-place mechanisms
    • Complex organic geometries
    • Topology-optimized forms

    Individual Assignment#

    Printer and Material Used#

    I used my personal Bambu Lab A1 3D printer along with WOL3D PLA filament.

    Filament Specifications:#

    • Print Temperature: 190°C – 220°C
    • Bed Temperature: 50–60°C
    • Shrinkage / Warping: Minimal
    • Diameter: 1.75 mm

    Since I am very familiar with both the printer and filament, the workflow was smooth. I used the default Generic PLA settings available in Bambu Studio.

    Items Designed and Printed#

    I designed and 3D printed three items this week.

    Item 1 – 3D Octagonal Lattice Structure#

    Design Process#

    1. Opened SolidWorks.
    2. Created a single octagonal unit cell based on my design choice.
    3. Imported the part into Assembly.
    4. Used linear patterning to replicate the cell and form the entire lattice structure.

    Design Considerations (Based on Group Assignment)#

    From our printer testing:

    • All bridge lengths were kept below 20 mm
    • All overhang angles were kept below 45°

    Why These Constraints Matter#

    Bridging (< 20 mm)#

    Bridging refers to printing across a gap without support underneath.

    If the bridge is too long:

    • Filament sags
    • Surface becomes rough
    • Structural integrity reduces

    By keeping bridge lengths under 20 mm:

    • The extruded filament could span the gap cleanly
    • Minimal drooping occurred
    • No supports were required

    Overhangs (< 45°)#

    Overhang angle is measured from vertical.

    • Below 45° → Each new layer is sufficiently supported by the previous layer.
    • Above 45° → The layer begins to print into air, causing sagging.

    By keeping overhang angles below 45°:

    • No support structures were required
    • Surface finish remained clean
    • Print time was reduced

    Why This Object Cannot Be Made Subtractively#

    This lattice structure contains:

    • Repeating internal geometry
    • Interconnected voids
    • Internal unsupported features

    A subtractive tool (like a milling cutter) cannot reach internal cavities without splitting the part into multiple pieces.

    Additive manufacturing enables this in a single print.

    Item 2 – Goblin Flexi Toy#

    While scrolling through Instagram, I came across an account called PIODEER:

    🔗 https://www.instagram.com/piodeer.stl/

    They had posted a reel about making a flexi toy, which inspired me.

    That gave me the idea to convert the Goblin model I created in Week 2 using Blender into a flexi toy.

    Initial Approach#

    • I reached out to the Instagram account admin.
    • They shared the STL file of the cutter used to make the flexi mechanism.

    After discussing this with my instructor Saheen, he asked me to:

    Design the cutter from scratch instead of directly using the provided file.

    Cutter Design Process#

    1. Studied the cutter design shared by PIODEER.
    2. Understood the mechanism logic.
    3. Drew inspiration from it.
    4. Designed my own cutter from scratch.

    Boolean Workflow in Bambu Studio#

    • Imported both STL files:
      • Goblin model → Positive body
      • Cutter model → Negative body
    • Used boolean merging:
      • Subtracted the cutter geometry from the goblin.
    • Generated the final flexi model.
    • Printed the result.

    Why This Cannot Be Made Subtractively#

    The flexi toy contains:

    • Print-in-place moving segments
    • Internal clearances
    • Interlocked geometry

    Such geometry would require:

    • Multiple part fabrication
    • Assembly
    • Additional fastening

    Additive manufacturing allows it to be printed as a single articulated object.

    Item 3 – Print-in-Place Engine Model#

    For the third item, my instructor Saheen asked me to design something that combines:

    • Clearances
    • Bridging
    • Print-in-place mechanisms

    Concept Selection#

    Initially, I considered designing a car model, but I decided to push further and create something more mechanically meaningful.

    I chose to design a simple engine model that includes:

    • Multiple moving parts
    • Tight clearances
    • Internal mechanisms assembled in a single print

    Design Process#

    • Used Fusion 360 in hybrid modeling mode
    • Designed multiple components as part of a single system:
      • Crankshaft
      • Piston
      • Connecting Rod
      • Outer Casing

    All components were designed such that they:

    • Exist within the same assembly
    • Have precise clearances to allow motion after printing
    • Do not require post-assembly

    Key Design Considerations#

    Clearances#

    • Small gaps were introduced between moving parts
    • Ensured parts do not fuse during printing
    • Critical for motion in:
      • Crank rotation
      • Piston sliding

    Bridging#

    • Internal features required unsupported spans
    • Bridge lengths were kept within tested limits (~20 mm)
    • Ensured clean formation without sagging

    Print-in-Place Mechanism#

    • Entire engine is printed as a single unit
    • No assembly required
    • Moving parts function immediately after print

    Why This Cannot Be Made Subtractively#

    • Internal mechanisms are enclosed within the casing
    • Tools cannot access internal features
    • Would require splitting into multiple parts and assembling

    This makes it a strong example of additive manufacturing advantage
